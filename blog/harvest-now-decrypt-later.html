Harvest Now, Decrypt Later: The $2 Trillion Risk

There's a class of attack that requires no quantum computer today but depends on quantum computers existing in the future. It's called "Harvest Now, Decrypt Later" (HNDL), and it's already happening.

The Attack

The strategy is straightforward: adversaries — particularly nation-state intelligence agencies — are collecting encrypted communications, signed transactions, and cryptographic material today. They store this data, waiting for quantum computers capable of running Shor's algorithm to break the underlying cryptography.

The data doesn't expire. A Bitcoin transaction signed with ECDSA in 2024 will be just as breakable in 2035 as it will be in 2045. The adversary has unlimited patience because the data has unlimited shelf life.

Why Crypto Is Uniquely Vulnerable

Most HNDL discussions focus on encrypted communications — diplomatic cables, military plans, corporate secrets. But cryptocurrency has a unique vulnerability: the blockchain is a permanent, public record of every transaction.

Every ECDSA signature on the Bitcoin blockchain is publicly available. An adversary doesn't need to intercept anything — they just download the blockchain. When quantum computers arrive, they can extract private keys from public signatures, enabling them to spend any remaining UTXO.

The total value at risk? Over $2 trillion in Bitcoin alone, plus hundreds of billions in Ethereum and other chains using ECDSA or EdDSA.

The Timeline Problem

NIST estimates cryptographically relevant quantum computers could arrive between 2030 and 2040. That sounds distant, but consider the timeline for migrating cryptographic infrastructure:

Identifying all systems using vulnerable algorithms takes months. Evaluating and selecting replacement algorithms takes months. Implementation, testing, and deployment takes months to years. Full ecosystem migration — including all counterparties, validators, and downstream systems — takes years.

If quantum computers arrive in 2032 and you start preparing in 2030, you're already too late. The organizations that start in 2025-2026 are the ones that will be ready.

What Phoenix Does About It

Phoenix Quantum Attestation addresses HNDL by creating post-quantum proof today. Every transaction that receives a Phoenix attestation has dual PQC signatures (ML-DSA + SLH-DSA) that will remain valid even after quantum computers arrive.

This is not about replacing ECDSA on the blockchain — that requires protocol-level changes. It's about creating an independent, quantum-safe attestation layer that proves a transaction was authorized by the legitimate signer, using algorithms that quantum computers cannot break.

When quantum computers arrive and ECDSA falls, transactions with Phoenix attestation have provable, quantum-safe authorization records. Transactions without it have only ECDSA — which will be broken.

Fiduciary Implications

For custodians managing assets on behalf of others, HNDL creates a fiduciary question. If the threat is known and mitigation is available, failing to act may constitute negligence. The exact legal landscape is still forming, but the direction is clear: quantum risk awareness is becoming a fiduciary requirement.
